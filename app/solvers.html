Position paper · Cross-chain solvers

Keep the speed. Lose the unconstrained key.

Solvers accept an order on one chain and deliver the outcome on another. To win, they need hot inventory and fast signatures. That same hot inventory, sitting behind a key that must stay online, is the largest loss surface in cross-chain. This paper argues the fix isn't slower solvers — it's signing that's gated by code, not by an operator. Settled is a working example.

01 The forcing function

The old cross-chain flow asked the user to do the routing: choose a bridge, choose a DEX, approve, wait, hope. Intent-based systems flipped it. The user states the outcome — "I have this on chain A, I want at least that on chain B" — and a market of solvers competes to deliver it. Better UX, better pricing, less for the user to get wrong.

But convenience moves the risk, it doesn't remove it. Someone now has to hold inventory on both sides and sign the transfers that fill the order. The faster that someone fills, the more inventory they keep hot and the more readily their keys must sign. The competitive pressure that makes solvers good is the same pressure that makes them dangerous to run.

02 The loss pattern

The headline risk in cross-chain isn't abstract "bridge risk." It's far more boring: inventory controlled by a key that has to be online to be useful. A leaked hot wallet, a compromised API key that can trigger a signer, an operator-side bug that releases a transfer it shouldn't — the asset is gone the moment the signature goes out.

If a compromised key can move your inventory, you don't have a custody model — you have a race between you and whoever gets the key first.

03 A taxonomy of solvers

"Solver" covers a wide range of designs. They differ in how orders arrive and how winners are chosen, but every one of them eventually answers the same question: who can move solver inventory, and under what conditions?

CategoryHow it fillsInventory control question
Fast-fill networksRelayer fronts the destination asset, settles later from a poolWhat stops the relayer key from fronting a fill that won't settle?
Dutch-auction resolversPrice decays until a solver takes the orderIs the winning signature checked against the order it claims to fill?
Batch-auction solversOrders cleared together at a uniform priceWho authorizes the batch settlement transfer?
RFQ makersMaker streams a firm quote, fills on acceptanceCan a stolen quoting credential also release funds?
Aggregators / routersSplit an order across venues and chainsHow many keys touch the path, and which can move value?
Settlement railsCarry the cross-chain message the fill depends onIs the source-chain fact verified before the destination pays?

The categories compete on latency and price. They converge on one operational truth: the value lives behind a signature, and the safety of the whole thing is the safety of whatever decides to release it.

04 Where the signatures live

Walk any solver's lifecycle and the risk localizes fast. Intake and selection move data. The later phases move money — and those are the signatures where a compromise becomes a loss.

Order intake

An intent arrives. No inventory moves. A leaked credential here leaks information, not funds.

Solver selection

The market decides who fills. Still no transfer — the danger is downstream of the winner.

Destination execution signs value

The solver pays the user on the destination chain. First place a key moves real inventory.

Claim / settlement signs value

The solver reclaims the source asset. Must be gated on the source-chain fact actually being true.

Rebalancing signs value

Inventory is moved back into position. Routine, frequent, and a favorite target.

Three of five phases are inventory-moving signatures. Securing a solver is, in practice, securing those three moments — making each one provably conditional on the order, the route, the amount, the deadline, and the source-chain truth.

05 The proposition: verifiable control

The reflex fix — multisig everything, add a human approval — trades away the latency that makes a solver competitive. The better fix keeps strategy fast and constrains only the one thing that matters: release of the signature.

Make signing programmable and attestable. The inventory sits behind code, not behind an operator. Before any transfer, that code checks the order, the route, the amount, the deadline, and profitability against the on-chain facts — and only then releases a signature. The code runs in a Trusted Execution Environment that produces evidence of what it approved or denied. The strategy can stay as fast as it likes; the key simply can't be talked into signing something the policy forbids.

  • Policy-gated inventory — funds release only when the order, amount, and deadline check out, evaluated by code rather than discretion.
  • Attested execution — authorization happens inside a TEE that yields an auditable record of each approval and denial.
  • Source-chain verification — destination payments and claims are conditioned on the source-chain state actually being what was claimed.
  • Role separation — quoting, strategy, execution, and emergency authority are split so a single leaked credential can't move funds.
  • Phase-aware strictness — the latency-sensitive fill and the slower claim can carry different gates without one weakening the other.

Keep the strategy fast. Gate the signature with code. Prove what the code decided.

06 Settled: one concrete implementation

Settled is a trustless cross-chain settlement protocol built exactly on this idea. It runs a two-sided market — users announce intents, solvers compete to fill them — and the inventory for each fill never sits behind an operator's hot wallet. It sits behind a Lit Action: code whose signing key is generated and used only inside a TEE, attested by a complete root-of-trust system. No one — not even Lit — can extract it.

How a fill is constrained

Each swap gets its own salt, which produces a unique IPFS CID, which derives a unique signing key and unique deposit addresses. The key is bound to the exact audited code at that CID. Before the user funds, the app recomputes the CID from the published template and compares — so a quote can only ever run the code it claims to. Funding one quote can never release another's key.

The same five gates, made concrete

  • Policy-gated inventory. The action reads all settlement parameters from the on-chain contract and asserts the delivered amount clears the user's floor (destAmount >= minDestAmount) before signing. A leaked API key can't widen those terms — the code, not the caller, decides.
  • Attested execution. Signing happens inside the TEE; the swap ends with a signed receipt carrying both tx hashes, amounts, and timestamps — evidence of what was settled.
  • Source-chain verification. Before settling either leg the action confirms both deposits are funded, so it never pays one side when the other can't be. UTXO and Zcash legs are held back from finalize until their settlement tx reaches the swap's confirmation depth — fail-closed: an unreachable explorer defers rather than assumes success.
  • Role separation. A four-address model gives every party its own address per chain: on success the source asset pays the solver and the dest asset pays the user; on expiry each side is refunded on its own chain. Settlement functions can only be called by the Lit Action's own address.
  • Phase-aware, idempotent steps. The action does one settlement step per invocation and logs each leg on-chain before the next. A crash, timeout, or sandbox kill mid-swap is recovered by re-invoking — the contract refuses to settle a leg twice, pay the fee twice, or finalize without both legs.

No bridges, no wrapped tokens, no HTLC complexity. Eleven chain pairs across EVM, Bitcoin, Litecoin, Dogecoin, Zcash, and Solana share one chain-agnostic settlement engine. EVM↔EVM (Base mainnet), EVM↔Bitcoin (signet), EVM↔Solana, and EVM↔Zcash (mainnet) have settled live end to end.

07 Proof, not promises

A mature solver shouldn't ask to be trusted — it should be able to show its work. The bar:

  • Inventory moved under verified policy, not operator discretion — and you can point to the code that enforced it.
  • Evidence that a risky request was genuinely denied, not merely never attempted.
  • Key material that cannot be exported or used outside the attested code path.
  • Execution tied to a code identity (here, an IPFS CID) and to on-chain facts, so any settlement step can be reconstructed after the fact.

Settled meets this by construction: every leg's tx hash is stored on the contract, the fee is logged independently of the legs, the final receipt is signed by the action's key, and the read-only Explorer renders each swap's full on-chain record — state, the four role addresses, deposit addresses, both legs, fee, and the action that custodied it.

08 Questions to ask any solver

The gap between "we're careful" and "the funds can't move unless code allows it" is the whole game. Six questions that expose it:

  1. If an attacker has your API keys, can they still move funds — or does code gate every transfer?
  2. Can you prove a risky or out-of-policy fill was denied, not just that it didn't happen?
  3. Is signing authority separated from strategy, so one leaked credential isn't game over?
  4. What reconstructs source-chain truth before a claim or withdrawal is signed?
  5. Can you show an auditor exactly which code authorized a given movement?
  6. Can the signing key be exported, or does it exist only inside attested execution?
